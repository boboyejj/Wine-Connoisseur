Do You Know How to Choose A Good Wine?¶

An analysis of wine reviews with location, variety, winery and vintage.

Author: Phoebe Tai, Jiejia Ye¶

1. Introduction¶

Have you ever been in a situation that you want to buy a bottle of wine for dinner or party, but you totally have no idea how to choose wine, especially facing the endless choices on the shelves? Most people are not familiar with wine. We may know their preference of flavors - sweet, fruity or sour. But that only after we taste the wine then we can say if we like or dislike it. So how can we choose a bottle of good wine?

Here, we will analyze a wine review dataset that contains more than 130k records including all information in the label on the bottle. Based on those information and visualizeing the relations between different features and rating of wine, we can learn some general ideas about the charateristic of good wine and find out what kind of wine is the most popular and worth to buy, especially high quality with low price. At last, we will use a machine learning model to help predict the rating of a bottle of wine based on the information on its label so that we can select a bottle of good wine based on the predict result based on the prediction. By using the prediction model, you will become a wine expert in no time and can pair a bottle of good wine with your feast.

Let's get started!

2.3 Data Cleaning¶

Here are the first five rows of the untouched dataset.

Most of the features are included in the wine bottle label. From the label, you will know the location where the grapes grew, the variety of grape, the winery name, and a description of the wine. Those all are useful information to help choose wine.

Right off the bat, we're going to eliminate the Taster Name and Twitter Handle columns, as we want to predict the quality of a wine based on production factors, not reviewers. And one would hope a taster's Twitter handle has no bearing on their opinion of a wine.

We are also going to drop the Description column. Although it would have been fun to run these through a natural language processor and see common characteristics of the highest scoring wines, these may be biased by the taster's impression of the wine.

Finally, we'll drop the Designation and Region 2 columns, which specify the exact vineyard and subregion in which the grapes are grown, respectively. These are too specific for our purposes, and, from the dataset description, they often have missing values. The rest of the columns may end up being useful to us, so we'll keep them for now.

There are quite a few things to note here. First, the wine scores have an approximately normal distribution, which is about what we'd expect. 4. Machine Learning¶

4.1 Random Forest Regression¶

Before we apply any machine learning models, we're going to need to process our data further. Because we are doing regression analysis, we need to ensure there is no multicollinearity between variables, as its presence may throw off our predictions. It is a reasonable (if not blindingly obvious) assumption that our location variables are perfectly correlated with one another. As such, we will choose to drop the Country, Region 1, and Winery columns. A wine's country of origin likely does not provide enough information to significantly improve our model. On the other hand, there may be too many distinct regions and wineries for those columns to be of much use. Settling with Province seems like a healthy compromise.

Next, we need to one-hot encode our data. This means converting all our categorical variables to binary ("dummy") variables, so that our algorithms know what to do with them! Luckily, this can be done in a single line.

We will also want to establish a baseline estimate, so we'll have something to compare our results against later. This will involve the highly scientific approach of averaging the points, then using that average as an estimate for every single wine in our dataset. If our machine learning models are unable to surpass this incredibly naive "algorithm", it's a sign that we need to reevaluate our approach.

Lastly, we need to split the data into training and testing sets. We'll start with the default 75:25 split, with 75% of our data being used for training, and the other 25% for testing. We can fine-tune these later if necessary.

In [0]:

labels = encoded['points']

# Split data 75:25 into train and test sets
train_features, test_features, train_labels, test_labels = train_test_split(features, labels)

We're finally ready to start. First, we'll use the most straightforward, time-tested approach: linear regression. We have no reason to suspect that the relationship between the independent variables and a wine's score is nonlinear, so this is a pretty safe choice.

This produces a model with a miserably low R-squared of 0.26, which implies our assumption of a linear model is off base. This may also be due to extreme outliers in the price and vintage, and in future analyses they should be removed from the dataset for more accurate predictions. For now, let's move on to the next method.

Let's try using a random forest to predict the ratings. Random forests are known for their high accuracy, and serve as a fairly efficient way to analyze large datasets such as ours. We initially started with 100 trees, but for the sake of everyone's time and sanity we have decreased this to 20.
